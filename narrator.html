The finding

Real feature or toy? Both, honestly — and the ratio depends on what "prose" is asked to carry. As a standup written by the graph this is close to real: the tense rules are a genuine, checkable read over the same kernel scher/test/mrs-dalloway.play.test.ts already proves, and a team that trusts its graph could plausibly read this instead of triaging fifteen cards by eye. As an accessibility voice-read it is closer to real than toy for the same reason: screen readers already want linear prose, and "closed, behind Now" IS more legible read aloud as "was mended" than as a checkbox and a timestamp. As the journal the boardview-archaeology talk wished for — a day you can read back years later and feel, not just audit — it's presently a toy: the sentences are topology-accurate but emotionally flat; a real journal needs texture (why it mattered, not just when it closed) that the graph doesn't carry and this muslin doesn't fake.

What breaks: prose order. Narrative wants causality order — the order a reader needs to make sense of consequence (grounds before what they ground, the telling before the party that received it) — not timestamp order. This muslin's ordering read is a topological sort over q-grounding edges reachable from each vantage's Now (a fixed-point walk seeded at the beats with no ungrounded parent within the shown set, breaking ties by witnessed-time only when causality is silent). It is NOT a general solution: two independent straddlers (the imperfective pair) have no causal order between them at all, so the muslin falls back to alphabetic-by-slug for ties, which is arbitrary and visibly so — Clarissa's paragraph and Septimus's paragraph could just as honestly print in the other order, and nothing in the graph says which reads first. A real version would need an explicit "which straddler does the reader meet first" rule, and that rule is authorial, not topological — the graph runs out before the prose does.
